Beginnings in the Quiet Corners of the Web

There was no formal classroom. No professor. Just a hand-me-down laptop, a late-night Google search, and a growing fascination with how websites worked.

My name is SEMER KELIL, and like many self-taught developers, my journey into web development didn’t start with a CS degree ,it started with curiosity. I stumbled upon the “Inspect Element” tool while customizing a Tumblr theme. That small moment lit a spark. One “how to make a website” YouTube video led to another. Before I knew it, I was knee-deep in HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

I didn’t know it then, but building my own Articles page would become a defining milestone in my journey—not just as a developer, but as a thinker.

The Idea Behind the Articles Page

At first, my website was just a résumé in disguise some projects, a "Hello World" homepage, and contact links. But it felt... empty. I wanted it to say more. To show more.

That’s when the idea hit me: “What if I could turn my learning process into content? What if my portfolio had a voice?”

I decided to create an Articles page a digital library of my thoughts, experiments, and breakthroughs. Not only would it be a personal journal of my progress, but it would also become a resource for others who were just a few steps behind me.

Writing as a Developer

I’m not a natural writer. I’m a thinker who types slowly. But something magical happens when you try to explain something to others:

“How I Finally Understood Flexbox”

I wrote it not because I mastered it, but because I struggled with it for weeks. That’s what made it real. Over time, my writing improved. My voice got clearer. My imposter syndrome softened every time someone DM’d me to say, “This helped me a lot, thanks!”

Topics I Covered:

  • Learning React useEffect the hard way.
  • My first freelance gig and what I wish I knew.
  • Why I stopped chasing 100% test scores on freeCodeCamp.
  • The difference between “knowing” and “debugging”.

Unexpected Opportunities

After launching my Articles page, something interesting happened:

People started finding me.

  • A recruiter messaged me on LinkedIn after reading my post about building a to-do app in React.
  • A fellow dev from India emailed to collaborate on a blog series.
  • My article was even shared in a Reddit thread about learning Flexbox.

That page, which started as a small corner of my site, became the beating heart of my portfolio. It wasn't just a showcase of code—it was a showcase of thought.

What I Learned Along the Way

1. You Don’t Have to Be an Expert to Teach

Write while you’re learning. Your struggles will help someone else who’s stuck.

2. Design Is Communication

A clean, readable layout matters more than flashy animations. Let your content breathe.

3. Consistency Beats Perfection

Publishing small posts consistently will beat waiting months for the “perfect” tutorial.

4. Learning in Public Builds Confidence

When you share, you grow faster. You open doors.

What’s Next?

I still don’t know everything. I still Google “how to center a div” sometimes. But now, I have a place where I can document that growth, inspire others, and remind myself how far I’ve come.

The Articles page?

It’s more than a blog.

It’s a mirror of my learning, a magnet for opportunity, and a message to others:

You don’t need permission to build. You just need momentum.

Final Words

If you’re a self-taught developer reading this: Start writing. Start sharing. Start building your Articles page. Don’t wait to become an “expert.” Let your journey be the content. Your story matters. Your site can be more than a portfolio. It can be your personal developer documentary and your Articles page is the narration.
